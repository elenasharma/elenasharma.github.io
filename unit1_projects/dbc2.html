The Bildungsroman

Or, the coming-of-age novel

So, what is it?

Ever noticed something in common in the stories told and re-told over time, whether in the form of fairy tales or more modern narratives like Star Wars and the Harry Potter series? Often, some of the most memorable stories follow the protagonist from childhood or innocence as they change in the face of the challenges of their worlds.

If you've ever gotten lost on TVTropes and most likely even if you haven't, you'll recognize the idea of the loss of innocence - an idea instrumental for the Bildungsroman genre. Scholar Marianne Hirsch breaks down the Bildungsroman into a set of seven conditions - conditions that can be used to classify texts as members of this long-standing and popular genre.

Of course, this is only one definition of the genre - there are countless subgenres within the Bildungsroman. These novels can satisfy all, some, or none of Hirsch's conditions. Some traditional examples of this genre include David Copperfield, Catcher in the Rye, and Jane Eyre, but the scope of the genre is always expanding to include novels like Divergent or movies like the original Star Wars trilogy.

The genre according to Hirsch...

  1. Focuses on a main character's development
  2. Is about society as well as the individual
  3. Is a quest for a meaningful existence
  4. Ultimately assesses protagonist's place in society
  5. Is characterized by irony toward protagonist
  6. Has other characters as educators, companions, lovers
  7. Is didactic, educating both reader and protagonist

Hirsch, Marianne. "The Novel of Formation as Genre: Between Great Expectations and Lost Illusions." Genre (Normal, Okla.) 12.1 (1979): 293-311. EBSCO. Web. 21 Feb. 2014.
